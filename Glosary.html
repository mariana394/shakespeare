GLOSARY







In a pickle



This phrase was used in “The Tempest”.


Meaning: Someone is or has been in a difficult position.


Example: I hate being in a pickle as it makes be frustrated and stressed.





Green-eyed monster


This phrase was used in “Othello”

Meaning:Jealousy. This is a powerful emotion that could drive crazy to someone, this emotion could be described as a monster. Also, green is normally associated with sickness or pain.

Example: A young man may suffer from the green-eyed monster if his girlfriend begins going out with someone else.






Love is blind


This phrase was used in “The Merchant of Venice”


Meaning:Used to say that people do not see the faults of the people they love.


Example:Everybody thought that Sam would not make a good match for Jill, but love is blind.






Bedazzled


This phrase was used in “The Taming of the Shrew”


Meaning:It means shiny and bright.


Example:My eyes have been so bedazzled with the sun.








Cold-blooded


This phrase was used in “King John”


Meaning:It is used to describe someone who is emotionally cold, a person that do not express his feelings.


Example:Prosecutors say the brothers are cold-blooded killers.






Rant


This phrase was used in “Hamlet”


Meaning:It can be used when someone speaks, writes, or shouts in an angry way and uncontrolled, often saying silly things.


Example:I get fed up with my mother ranting and raving all the time.






Assassination


This phrase was used in “Macbeth”


Meaning:It refers to the murder of someone famous or important by sudden or a secret attack.


Example:The assassination of the Archduke Ferdinand started off the first World War.






Fashionable


This phrase was used in “Troilus and Cressida”

Meaning:We now used it to refer to something popular at a particular time, Shakespeare used it to refer that someone was up with the times, like if time was a good host.

Example:It's not fashionable to wear crocs at the moment.








Obscene


This phrase was used in “Love’s Labour’s Lost”

Meaning: It first meant that something was offensive to our senses, could be to our sight, smell, taste, etc. That meaning changed and now it means anything that we find offensive in general.

Example: Somebody's been posting obscene messages in this WhatsApp chat.





Addiction


This phrase was used in “Henry V”.

Meaning:First, it meant a tendency or an inclination for something, it was until the twentieth century that it took a different connotation. Now it means an inability to stop doing or using something.

Example: I have an addiction to mystery stories.






Swagger


This phrase was used in “A Midsummer Night’s Dream”.


Meaning:It meant walking in a proud way trying to look important, the meaning has not change much, but it sometimes used in a different connotation.


Example:A group of teenagers swaggered around outside the bar.






Zany


This phrase was used in “Love’s Labour’s Lost”.


Meaning:To say something is strange or surprising in a humorous way, it can also be used to describe a person as a clown or crazy.


Example:Joshua made us all laugh with his zany tricks.







Knock, Knock! Who’s there?


This phrase was used in “Macbeth”

Meaning:Nowadays we use this phrase as a classic set up for a joke but none of the jokes, we tell now are close to what happened in Macbeth’s story. It is ironic that we have taken a line from a scene discussing murder, and politics and made it into a joke for kids.

Example:Knock, knock. Who’s there? Atch. Atch who? Bless you!






Good riddance!


This phrase was used in “Troilus and Cressida”


Meaning:Said to express relief at being free of a troublesome or unwanted person or thing.


Example:Our neighbors and their dogs have moved out – good riddance!






Blanket


This word was used in “King Lear”


Meaning:To cover with something.


Example:Outside the fields were blanketed in fog.






Hint


This word was used in “Othello”

Meaning:Something that you say or do that shows what you think or want in a way that is not direct.

Example:Did she give you any hints about where she was going?








Lonely


This word was used in “Coriolanus”

Meaning: Unhappy because you are not with other people.

Example: I've been lonely since my friends moved away.





Torture


This word was used in “Henry IV, Part 2”

Meaning:To cause great physical or mental pain to someone intentionally.

Example: It is claimed that the officers tortured a man to death in a city police station.






Critic


This word was used in “Love’s labour’s Lost”


Meaning:Someone who expresses disagreement with something or disapproval of someone.


Example:Her critics say she is leading the party to disaster.






Gossip


This word was used in “The Comedy of Errors”

Meaning:First, it meant to speak within a familiar way, but now it means to have a conversation about other people's private lives that might be unkind.

Example:Batman and Superman sat in the kitchen having a good gossip about their enemies.







Bloody


This word was used in “Henry IV, Part 2”

Meaning:Used to express anger or to emphasize what you are saying in a rude way.


Example:I had a bloody good time last night.






Hot-blooded


This phrase was used in “King Lear”


Meaning:It was first used to refer something about passion or lust, nowadays is used to talk about anger, someone who has quite a temper.


Example:Rock stars seem to be perpetually followed by hot-blooded groupies.






Mimic


This word was used in “A Midsummer Night’s Dream”


Meaning:To copy the way a person usually speaks and moves.


Example:Aaron could mimic all the teachers' accents.






Malignant


This word was used in “Henry VI Part 1”


Meaning:Having a strong wish to make harm.


Example:A malignant attitude will turn others away from friendship.








Dawn


This word was used in “Henry V”


Meaning: Period in the day when light from the sun begins to appear in the sky.


Example: We woke at dawn.






Pedant


This word was used in “The Taming of the Shrew”

Meaning:Apply to a teacher or bookish type of person, interested in formal rules and small details.

Example: The pedant likes to chant poems before his students in rhythmic measured tones.





Seen better days


This phrase was used in “As you Like it”


Meaning:Being in a poor condition.


Example:My computer has seen better days so I’m thinking about getting a new one.





Neither rhyme nor reason


This phrase was used in “The Comedy of Errors”


Meaning:Without common sense or logic.


Example:John’s letter doesn’t make sense; it is neither rhyme nor reason.







Cruel to be kind


This phrase was used in “Hamlet”


Meaning:Tough love, being harsh for their benefit.


Example:I told her she wasn't good enough to be a professional dancer, sometimes you have to be cruel to be kind.





Clothes make the man


This phrase was used in “Hamlet”


Meaning:People are judged by the way they dress.


Example:You should wear your best suit if you are going for a job interview, the clothes make the man.





Own flesh and blood


This phrase was used in “Hamlet”


Meaning:Part of my family, people that share DNA.


Example:Of course I care about her well-being. My sister is my flesh and blood after all.





A dish fit for the gods


This phrase was used in “Julius Caesar”


Meaning:A high-quality meal.


Example:I ate this delicious vegetarian dish at Sophia’s new restaurant. It was a dish fit for the gods.







What’s done is done.


This phrase was used in “Macbeth”


Meaning: We can’t change what has happened in the past.


Example: Oh no! I broke my moms’ new lamp, well, what’s done is done.





Not slept one wink


This phrase was used in “Cymbeline”


Meaning:Not sleep at all.


Example: The hotel was so noisy, I didn't get a wink of sleep.






It’s Greek to me


This phrase was used in “The Comedy of Errors”


Meaning:- It's unintelligible, I cannot understand


Example:My teacher was explaining a complicated math problem to the classroom, but I didn’t get it, it was all Greek to me.





Wear my heart upon my sleeve


This phrase was used in “Othello”


Meaning:To expose my feelings, be vulnerable, that someone is displaying emotion openly.


Example:You’d never be good a poker. You always wear your heart on your sleeve.

Break the ice


This phrase was used in “Taming of the shrew”


Meaning:To reduce the awkward, initial social tension.


Example:The party was boring until someone broke the ice with a joke.






Lackluster


This word was used in “As you like it”


Meaning:Without energy and effort.


Example:I looked at my friend with lackluster eyes because he betrayed me.






Generous


This word was used in “Love’s Labour’s Lost”

Meaning:Shakespeare meant someone high-minded or noble, but now it is used to describe someone willing to give help or support.

Example:The charity will go under unless a generous donor can be found.
